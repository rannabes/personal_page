Introduction

The following mini-tutorials provides a simple and yet good-enough definitione of the most commonly used concepts in quantum algorithms. I am preparing these tutorials for audience with zero or little in physics. Therefore, the definitions may not be as comprehensive or presice as the standard textbooks but they provide basic understanding for the reader to understand these concepts while they read an article on quantum computing or they would like to play with one of the commonly sued quantum softwares.

I hope this page be used as a "mini-wikipedia" for people with limited time. I provide two version, mathematical definition and and physical interpretation, depending on which way is easier for the reader to understand.

Quantum State

-Mathematical Description

An ideal qubit is a two level system whose state is zero or one or any arbitrary superposition of both. An ideal qubit is described by a "pure state" which mathematically is represented by a vector: $$\vert \psi \rangle = \alpha \vert 0 \rangle + \beta \vert 1 \rangle \hskip 1cm where \hskip 1cm \vert 0 \rangle = \begin{pmatrix} 1\\ 0 \end{pmatrix} \hskip 0.5 cm, \vert 1 \rangle = \begin{pmatrix} 0\\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$$ In practice, no qubit is ideal. It can be noisy or it can be part of a larger system. So, their state is not "pure". The mathematical description of qubits with mixed states is: $$ \rho = \frac{1}{2} \begin{pmatrix} 1 + a_z & a_x + i a_y \\ a_x - i a_y & 1 - a_z \end{pmatrix} \hskip 1.5cm or \hskip 1.5cm \rho = \frac{1}{2} (\mathbb{1} + a_x \ \sigma_x +a_y \ \sigma_y+a_z\ \sigma_z) $$ Here, \( (\alpha\ ,\ \beta) \) are complex numebrs, \( \{ a_{j}\} \) are real numbers and \( \{ \sigma_{j}\} \) are Pauli operators.
